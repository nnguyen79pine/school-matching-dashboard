EducatEd Choice

A Data-Driven School Matching System

Project Overview

EducatEd Choice helps families compare schools using data-driven insights. Instead of looking at one metric at a time, this project combines multiple academic and structural factors to create a clearer picture of school quality.

Data Processing and Data Model

Data from multiple sources was cleaned and standardized by removing duplicates, handling missing values, and aligning column formats.

The final dataset includes key school-level features such as: graduation rate, SAT total score, HOPE eligibility percentage, cohort size, mobility rate, mobility count, and discipline percentage.

These variables were used in Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to create two main dimensions: academic performance and school stability. These dimensions power the school matching system.

Statistical Analysis

To understand what actually drives school performance in Gwinnett County, we ran a full statistical analysis across both the NCES and USNews datasets. The goal was to move beyond surface-level comparisons and identify which factors are genuinely connected to outcomes like graduation rate, SAT scores, and HOPE eligibility.

Note on outlier exclusion: One school in the dataset, McClure Health HS, an alternative school serving students who have left traditional high schools, was excluded from all analyses below. This school had a mobility rate of 48.8% (the district average is 6.4%) and a graduation rate of 52.7%, both extreme outliers relative to every other school in the district. Including it would distort correlations, averages, and distributions in ways that do not reflect the typical Gwinnett high school experience. Because this school serves a fundamentally different student population under a different operational model, comparing it directly to comprehensive high schools would be statistically misleading.

Correlation Matrix

The correlation matrix below shows the pairwise relationships between all key numeric variables across matched schools. Values close to 1 or −1 indicate a strong relationship; values near 0 suggest little to no linear association.

Several relationships stood out. Mobility rate and graduation rate had one of the strongest correlations in the dataset at r = −0.72, meaning schools with high student turnover consistently had lower graduation rates. Disadvantaged percentage was strongly negatively correlated with both math proficiency (r = −0.72) and SAT total (r = −0.78), confirming that socioeconomic factors are closely tied to academic outcomes. Interestingly, discipline percentage was positively correlated with graduation rate (r = 0.58), the opposite of what was initially hypothesized, likely because larger, higher-performing schools generate more discipline incidents by sheer volume.

Mobility Rate vs. Graduation Rate

The scatter plot below isolates the mobility-graduation relationship. Schools with low mobility rates (under 7%) clustered tightly in the 90–99% graduation range, while schools with higher mobility were spread much lower.

This suggests that student stability is one of the most important predictors of whether students actually finish high school. It also raises questions about what is driving high mobility at certain schools and whether targeted intervention could close that gap.

Key Metric Comparisons by Subgroup

We split schools into subgroups based on their median values for graduation rate, mobility rate, and HOPE eligibility, then compared average outcomes across groups. The chart below shows average graduation rate and HOPE eligibility side by side for each subgroup.

Schools in the high graduation rate group averaged noticeably higher HOPE eligibility than low graduation rate schools. The high mobility group had the lowest HOPE eligibility of any subgroup, reinforcing that mobility affects not just graduation but college readiness as well.

Distribution Analysis

Understanding the shape of each metric's distribution matters for interpreting averages correctly. The histograms below show graduation rate, SAT total, HOPE eligibility, and mobility rate across the 23 schools included in the analysis.

Graduation rate and SAT total are both left-skewed, with most schools clustered at higher values and a small number of underperformers pulling the mean down. Mobility rate is right-skewed, making the median a more honest summary statistic than the mean for that metric.
